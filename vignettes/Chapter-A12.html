Chapter A12: Details for the Priors returned by the Prior_Setup Function

1. Introductory Discussion

This appendix records precise formulas and derivations for the prior objects returned by Prior_Setup() and for the conjugate Normal–Gamma Gaussian model used by dNormal_Gamma(). The goal is to connect implementation quantities (mu, Sigma, Sigma_0, dispersion, shape, rate, and related settings) to the weighted likelihood notation and \(S_{\mathrm{marg}}\) machinery in Chapter 11 (especially Section 3.2 and Appendix A3), with steps spelled out rather than only stated.

This chapter is a companion to the main vignettes: it emphasizes theory, mapping to pfamily constructors, and how defaults encode prior strength.

Roadmap. Chapter 11 fixes notation for weighted Gaussian regression (\(n_w\), \(G = X^{\mathsf T} W X\), precision \(\tau = 1/\phi\), and the conjugate Normal–Gamma structure). Appendix A3 there gives closed-form posterior moments for \(\beta\) under the Zellner-type prior implied by scalar pwt. Chapter A02 documents how pfamily objects map to lower-level simulation functions. Here we tie those ideas to what Prior_Setup() actually returns and how to pass those fields into dNormal(), dNormal_Gamma(), and dIndependent_Normal_Gamma() without mixing coefficient-scale Sigma, dispersion-free Sigma_0, and optional fixed dispersion (see ?Prior_Setup, ?compute_gaussian_prior).

2. Default Priors for Coefficient Means and Covariance Matrices

This section concerns families such as binomial and Poisson where the usual exponential-family dispersion is \(\phi=1\) (Chapters 5, 7, and 8). Gaussian models and dNormal_Gamma are in Section 3.

Let \(n_w = \sum_i w_i\) for nonnegative observation weights \(w_i\) in the weighted likelihood (the same totals appear as PriorSettings$n_effective). These \(w_i\) are fixed by design and do not depend on \(\beta\).

2.1 How prior means are determined

Unless you pass mu explicitly, Prior_Setup() fills mu from intercept_source and effects_source:

  • Intercept: from the intercept-only fit (intercept_source = "null_model", default) or from the full-model estimate ("full_model").
  • Other coefficients: zero (effects_source = "null_effects", default) or the full-model estimate ("full_model").

User-supplied mu replaces this (after dimension checks).

2.2 Data precision \(P(\beta)\).

Let \(\ell(\beta)\) be the weighted log-likelihood as in Chapters 7–8, with \(\eta_i = x_i^{\mathsf T}\beta\). Define the data precision matrix \[ P(\beta) := \nabla^2_\beta\bigl(-\ell(\beta)\bigr), \] the Hessian of the negative log-likelihood. With \(\ell(\beta)=\sum_i \ell_i(\eta_i)\), \[ P(\beta) = X^{\mathsf T} W(\beta)\, X, \qquad W_i(\beta) := -\frac{d^2 \ell_i}{d\eta_i^2}\Big|_{\eta_i=x_i^{\mathsf T}\beta} \ge 0 \] (log-concavity in \(\eta\); Chapter 5), and \(W(\beta)\) diagonal.

Write \(W_i(\beta) = w_i\,\omega_i(\beta)\) with fixed \(w_i\) and mean-dependent \(\omega_i(\beta)\). Let \(W_{\mathrm{obs}}=\mathrm{diag}(w_i)\) and \(\Omega(\beta)=\mathrm{diag}(\omega_i(\beta))\). Then \(W(\beta) = W_{\mathrm{obs}}\,\Omega(\beta)\) (indexwise) and \[ P(\beta) = X^{\mathsf T} W_{\mathrm{obs}}\,\Omega(\beta)\, X. \]

Examples (Chapters 7–8):

  • Poisson, log link: \(P(\beta) = X^{\mathsf T}\,\mathrm{diag}\bigl(w_i\,\mu_i(\beta)\bigr)\, X\).
  • Binomial, logit: \(P(\beta) = X^{\mathsf T}\,\mathrm{diag}\bigl(w_i\,\mu_i(\beta)(1-\mu_i(\beta))\bigr)\, X\).

2.3 Zellner-type prior using \(P(\beta^{\ast})\)

2.3.1 Precision mapping and default covariance scaling

For these families, Prior_Setup() sets dispersion, shape, rate, and Sigma_0 to NULL. Let \(V_0\) denote the sampling covariance matrix of the fitted coefficients \(\beta^{\ast}\) under the stated model (\(\phi=1\)). Then \[ V_0^{-1} = P(\beta^{\ast}). \]

Weighted Gaussian, fixed dispersion \(d\). (See Section 3 for prior outputs.) Then \(P(\beta)=\frac{1}{d} X^{\mathsf T} W_{\mathrm{obs}} X\) for all \(\beta\), and the same identification gives \(V_0^{-1}=\frac{1}{d} X^{\mathsf T} W_{\mathrm{obs}} X\) when \(V_0\) is the covariance matrix at dispersion \(d\). User-provided \(d\) in compute_gaussian_prior() sets returned dispersion to \(d\) and rescales Sigma so this scale is explicit in the returned list.

Prior covariance: scalar pwt, \(\Sigma = \frac{1-\mathrm{pwt}}{\mathrm{pwt}}\, V_0\) (equivalently \(\Sigma^{-1} = \frac{\mathrm{pwt}}{1-\mathrm{pwt}}\, P(\beta^{\ast})\))

This Sigma is what Prior_Setup() returns by default on the coefficient scale. For Gaussian fits, the returned dispersion-free matrix is

\[ \Sigma_0 = \Sigma / d, \] so \[ \Sigma_0^{-1} = d\,\Sigma^{-1} = d\,\frac{\mathrm{pwt}}{1-\mathrm{pwt}}\,P(\beta^{\ast}). \] Using \(P(\beta^{\ast})=\frac{1}{d}X^{\mathsf T}WX\) in weighted Gaussian regression gives \[ \Sigma_0^{-1} = \frac{\mathrm{pwt}}{1-\mathrm{pwt}}\,X^{\mathsf T}WX, \] which is independent of \(d\); this is the default Sigma_0 returned by Prior_Setup().

2.3.2 Posterior mean under dNormal()

When default settings are used, the Gaussian posterior means reduce to simple weighted averages of the fitted coefficient vector \(\beta^\ast\) and prior mean \(\mu\).

dNormal() (Gaussian, coefficient-scale covariance Sigma). For Gaussian likelihood precision \(P(\beta^\ast)\) and prior precision \(\Sigma^{-1}\), \[ E(\beta\mid y) = \bigl(P(\beta^\ast)+\Sigma^{-1}\bigr)^{-1} \Bigl(P(\beta^\ast)\beta^\ast+\Sigma^{-1}\mu\Bigr). \] With the default scalar pwt, \[ \Sigma^{-1} = \frac{\mathrm{pwt}}{1-\mathrm{pwt}}\,P(\beta^\ast), \] so \[ \begin{aligned} E(\beta\mid y) &= \left(P(\beta^\ast)+\frac{\mathrm{pwt}}{1-\mathrm{pwt}}P(\beta^\ast) \right)^{-1} \left(P(\beta^\ast)\beta^\ast+\frac{\mathrm{pwt}}{1-\mathrm{pwt}}P(\beta^\ast)\mu\right) \\ &= \left(\frac{1-\mathrm{pwt}}{1-\mathrm{pwt}}P(\beta^\ast)+\frac{\mathrm{pwt}}{1-\mathrm{pwt}}P(\beta^\ast) \right)^{-1} \left(\frac{1-\mathrm{pwt}}{1-\mathrm{pwt}}P(\beta^\ast)\beta^\ast+\frac{\mathrm{pwt}}{1-\mathrm{pwt}}P(\beta^\ast)\mu\right) \\ &= \left(\frac{1}{1-\mathrm{pwt}}P(\beta^\ast)\right)^{-1} \left(\frac{1}{1-\mathrm{pwt}}P(\beta^\ast)\bigl((1-\mathrm{pwt})\beta^\ast+\mathrm{pwt}\mu\bigr)\right) \\ &= (1-\mathrm{pwt})\beta^\ast+\mathrm{pwt}\,\mu. \end{aligned} \]

2.3.3 Marginal posterior mean under dNormal_Gamma()

dNormal_Gamma() (Gaussian conjugate Normal–Gamma, using Sigma_0). The marginal posterior mean is \[ E(\beta\mid y) = E_{\tau\mid y}\!\left[E(\beta\mid \tau,y)\right]. \] For fixed \(\tau\), \[ E(\beta\mid \tau,y) = \bigl(\tau X^{\mathsf T}W_{\mathrm{obs}}X+\tau\Sigma_0^{-1}\bigr)^{-1} \Bigl(\tau X^{\mathsf T}W_{\mathrm{obs}}X\,\beta^\ast+\tau\Sigma_0^{-1}\mu\Bigr). \] Under the default scalar pwt calibration for Sigma_0, \[ \Sigma_0^{-1} = \frac{\mathrm{pwt}}{1-\mathrm{pwt}}\,X^{\mathsf T}W_{\mathrm{obs}}X, \] so \[ \begin{aligned} E(\beta\mid \tau,y) &= \left(\tau X^{\mathsf T}W_{\mathrm{obs}}X+\tau\frac{\mathrm{pwt}}{1-\mathrm{pwt}}X^{\mathsf T}W_{\mathrm{obs}}X\right)^{-1} \left(\tau X^{\mathsf T}W_{\mathrm{obs}}X\,\beta^\ast+\tau\frac{\mathrm{pwt}}{1-\mathrm{pwt}}X^{\mathsf T}W_{\mathrm{obs}}X\,\mu\right) \\ &= \left(\frac{\tau}{1-\mathrm{pwt}}X^{\mathsf T}W_{\mathrm{obs}}X\right)^{-1} \left(\frac{\tau}{1-\mathrm{pwt}}X^{\mathsf T}W_{\mathrm{obs}}X\bigl((1-\mathrm{pwt})\beta^\ast+\mathrm{pwt}\mu\bigr)\right) \\ &= (1-\mathrm{pwt})\beta^\ast+\mathrm{pwt}\,\mu. \end{aligned} \] Because this expression is free of \(\tau\), averaging over \(\tau\mid y\) gives \[ E(\beta\mid y)=(1-\mathrm{pwt})\beta^\ast+\mathrm{pwt}\,\mu. \] For general non-Gaussian GLMs these equalities are not exact in finite samples, because the likelihood is not exactly quadratic in \(\beta\); however, the same weighted-average form is often a good approximation when the likelihood is close to multivariate normal, as typically occurs in large samples.

2.4 Vector pwt and optional sd

Vector pwt: same Hadamard construction as above; correlations in \(V_0\) are preserved, variances scaled per coordinate.

sd: \(\mathrm{pwt}_j = (V_0)_{jj}/\bigl((V_0)_{jj}+\mathrm{sd}_j^2\bigr)\); vector pwt is not overwritten from scalar n_prior. Gaussian fits may require scalar n_prior in addition (Section 3).

3. Default Priors for Dispersion, Shape, and Rate Parameters

3.1 Sigma_0 as an unscaled Zellner \(g\)-type scale

In Chapter 11 Section 3.2, the conjugate prior writes \(\beta \mid \tau \sim N(\mu_0, (\tau \Sigma_0)^{-1})\) with \(\Sigma_0\) free of the residual variance factor \(\phi = 1/\tau\): dispersion enters through \(\tau\) multiplying \(\Sigma_0^{-1}\).

In Prior_Setup(), \(\Sigma_{\mathrm{pre}}\) is built from \(V_0\) and pwt by the same Zellner rule as in Section 2.3 and lives on the coefficient data scale (it scales like \(V_0\)). Dividing by \(d_{\mathrm{OLS}}\) yields \(\Sigma_0^{\mathrm{(in)}} = \Sigma_{\mathrm{pre}} / d_{\mathrm{OLS}}\), which is the dispersion-free matrix fed to compute_gaussian_prior(). For scalar pwt, \[ \Sigma_0^{\mathrm{(in)}} = \frac{1-\mathrm{pwt}}{\mathrm{pwt}}\, \frac{V_0}{d_{\mathrm{OLS}}} \quad\text{and}\quad V_0 \approx d_{\mathrm{OLS}}\, G^{-1} \Rightarrow \Sigma_0^{\mathrm{(in)}} \approx \frac{1-\mathrm{pwt}}{\mathrm{pwt}}\, G^{-1}, \] matching the Zellner \(g\)-prior relative to \(G^{-1}\) in Chapter 11 Appendix A3. The returned Sigma_0 from Prior_Setup() is the helper’s dispersion-free matrix (possibly reconciled when sd or user dispersion / Sigma overrides apply; see below). Always pass Sigma_0 = ps$Sigma_0 into dNormal_Gamma(), not ps$Sigma alone.

3.2 How shape and rate are set

Role. The returned shape and rate define a Gamma prior on residual precision \(\tau\) in the shape–rate parameterization used elsewhere in this package (see Chapter 11). They are chosen jointly with the Gaussian calibration so that (i) the Normal–Gamma coupling is internally consistent and (ii) weak-prior limits align with classical weighted dispersion and covariance heuristics—an empirical-Bayes-style use of \(\hat\beta\), \(\mathrm{RSS}_w\), and \(n_w\) to set scales, while \(n_{\mathrm{prior}}\) (or scalar pwt) controls strength.

Shape (shape_df). Let \(p\) be the number of coefficients. With scalar n_prior and a family that supplies a numeric dispersion before the Gaussian overwrite (Gaussian or Gamma), the numerator \(n_{\mathrm{shape}}\) is \[ n_{\mathrm{shape}} \in \{n_{\mathrm{prior}},\; n_{\mathrm{prior}}+p,\; n_{\mathrm{prior}}-p\} \] according to shape_df ("n_prior", "n_prior+p", "n_prior-p"). The shape hyperparameter is \(n_{\mathrm{shape}}/2\). The "n_prior-p" case requires \(n_{\mathrm{prior}} > p\) so that shape stays positive (see ?Prior_Setup for the conjugate vs independent Normal–Gamma interpretation).

Calibration pipeline (compute_gaussian_prior()). Given \(G^{-1}\) from the weighted Gram matrix, \(\Sigma_0\), \(\hat\beta\), and \(\mu\), define \[ S_{\mathrm{marg}} = \mathrm{RSS}_w + (\hat\beta - \mu)^{\mathsf T} \bigl(\Sigma_0 + G^{-1}\bigr)^{-1} (\hat\beta - \mu). \] The helper sets (with \(n_w =\) n_effective) \[ \mathrm{dispersion} = S_{\mathrm{marg}}\, \frac{n_w + n_{\mathrm{prior}}}{n_w\,(n_w + n_{\mathrm{prior}} - 2)}, \qquad \mathrm{rate} = \frac{1}{2}\,\frac{n_{\mathrm{prior}}}{n_w}\, S_{\mathrm{marg}}, \] and \[ \Sigma_{\mathrm{calibrated}} = \frac{n_w}{n_{\mathrm{prior}}}\, \mathrm{dispersion}\, G^{-1}, \] while recomputing shape from the same shape_df rule. These are the values Prior_Setup() installs for Gaussian models when the calibration branch runs.

Limiting behavior (scalar pwt, conceptual defaults)

  • (a) Coefficients. The posterior mean of \(\beta\) in the conjugate Gaussian model is the precision blend \(E(\beta \mid y) = (1-\mathrm{pwt})\hat\beta + \mathrm{pwt}\,\mu_0\) (Chapter 11 A3.2); the returned coefficients vector implements this (and the elementwise extension for vector pwt).
  • (b) Dispersion / variance. For inverse precision, the marginal Gamma posterior gives \(E(\tau^{-1} \mid y) = b_n/(a_n-1)\) when \(a_n > 1\), with \(b_n = b_0 + \tfrac{1}{2} S_{\mathrm{marg}}\) in Chapter 11 notation. The calibrated dispersion returned by Prior_Setup() equals the closed-form \(E(\sigma^2 \mid y)\) expression in ?compute_gaussian_prior under the special calibration displayed above. As \(n_{\mathrm{prior}} \to 0^+\) with \(n_w > 2\), that mean dispersion ties to \(S_{\mathrm{marg}}/(n_w - 2)\) as documented there and in Chapter 11’s weak-prior discussion.
  • (c) Precision. For the marginal posterior on \(\tau\), one has \(E(\tau \mid y) = a_n / b_n\) in the same shape–rate parameterization (when \(b_n > 0\)). This is distinct from the coefficient mean in (a): it summarizes residual precision after seeing data under the chosen prior hyperparameters shape and rate (denoted \(a_0\) and \(b_0\) in Chapter 11).

Use Chapter 11 (especially Sections 3.2 and Appendix A3) for the full posterior identities and for the independent Normal–Gamma comparison when the same shape implies different weak limits (shape_df bullet list in ?Prior_Setup).

Mapping example.

ps <- Prior_Setup(formula, data, family = gaussian(), n_prior = n0)
lmb(
  formula,
  data,
  pfamily = dNormal_Gamma(
    ps$mu,
    Sigma_0 = ps$Sigma_0,
    shape     = ps$shape,
    rate      = ps$rate
  )
)

For matrix-input workflows, Chapter A02: prior_list for rNormalGamma_reg() uses Sigma = ps$Sigma_0 (dispersion-free slot) together with shape and rate.

3.3 Changes when pwt is a vector and when sd is provided

With vector pwt, \(\Sigma_{\mathrm{pre}}\) uses the Hadamard scaling in Section 2.4; \(\Sigma_0^{\mathrm{(in)}}\) divides that matrix by \(d_{\mathrm{OLS}}\) entrywise in the usual matrix sense. The blend for coefficients is elementwise \((1-\mathrm{pwt}_j)\hat\beta_j + \mathrm{pwt}_j \mu_j\).

With sd, pwt is computed from diagonals of \(V_0\) as in Section 2.4 and the coefficient-scale \(\Sigma_{\mathrm{pre}}\) from Step 8 is passed as Sigma into compute_gaussian_prior(). The helper then fixes returned Sigma to that matrix, sets dispersion from the user input when supplied, and sets \(\Sigma_0 = \Sigma / d\) for the returned dispersion scalar \(d\) (see ?compute_gaussian_prior).

3.4 Changes when Sigma is provided

User-supplied prior covariance at the compute_gaussian_prior() layer is handled via its Sigma argument (wired from Prior_Setup() when sd is used, or for direct calls). When Sigma is non-NULL, the returned objects satisfy Sigma_0 = Sigma / dispersion with returned dispersion either calibrated or user-provided, so that Sigma and Sigma_0 stay consistent for dNormal versus dNormal_Gamma. When only dispersion is supplied (no Sigma input), returned Sigma is Sigma_0 * dispersion. Details and edge cases are in ?compute_gaussian_prior.

4. Other Models With Unknown Dispersion

This appendix focused on the Prior_Setup() \(\to\) dNormal_Gamma() Gaussian path. Other compound specifications reuse parts of the same output but expect different slots:

  • dIndependent_Normal_Gamma() takes undivided ps$Sigma (coefficient scale) plus the same shape and rate; do not substitute Sigma_0 for Sigma there.
  • dGamma() with fixed coefficients uses shape, rate, and typically coefficients from the prior list for residual precision updates.

See Chapter A02 for prior_list templates (rNormalGamma_reg() vs rindepNormalGamma_reg()), Chapter 11 Section 3.3 for the independent Normal–Gamma model, and Chapter A07 for truncated-Gamma / envelope extensions.
